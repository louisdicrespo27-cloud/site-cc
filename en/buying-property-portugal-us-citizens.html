Buying Property in Portugal as a US Citizen: Legal Guide

United States citizens who wish to buy, sell or manage property in Portugal often find that the process follows rules and institutions unfamiliar from home. Land registry, notarial deeds, tax identification and contract formalities are governed by Portuguese law, regardless of where the owner lives.

This page outlines, in general terms, legal steps that commonly arise for American buyers: why local legal support is useful, obtaining a NIF, fiscal representation, buying at distance with a power of attorney, due diligence before commitment, and what to bear in mind on a later sale. It does not replace advice on your specific case.

Why a US buyer may need legal support in Portugal

Property transactions in Portugal do not follow the same structure as in most US states. There is no escrow-style closing in the American sense; instead, the process typically involves a promissory purchase contract (contrato-promessa de compra e venda, often referred to as CPCV), followed by a public deed (escritura) before a notary, with registration in the land registry (registo predial).

Each stage has formal requirements, deadlines and documents that may not be visible from listings or agent communications alone. For a buyer based in the United States, misunderstanding a clause, a missing licence or an encumbrance on the property can delay completion or create problems after purchase.

Independent legal review before signing helps clarify what is being acquired, what conditions apply and what risks the documentation reveals.

Portuguese tax number (NIF) for US citizens

A Portuguese tax identification number (NIF — número de identificação fiscal) is generally required to buy property, pay stamp duty and other transaction taxes, and handle ongoing tax communications relating to the asset. US citizens who are not tax-resident in Portugal can usually request a NIF through the channels provided for non-residents, which may include appointment at a tax office or use of a representative.

The NIF should be obtained early enough to align with the transaction timetable. Delays in tax registration can hold up signing, payment of taxes or registration steps that depend on the buyer being identified in the Portuguese system.

Whether additional tax registrations or declarations are needed later depends on how the property is used. That assessment is case-specific.

Fiscal representative in Portugal

Non-residents who own property or have tax matters in Portugal may need to appoint a fiscal representative (representante fiscal) — a person or entity authorised to receive communications from the Portuguese tax authority on the owner’s behalf.

Appointing a fiscal representative is, as a rule, required for residents outside the EU/EEA, subject to limited exceptions. US citizens should treat this as a step to clarify before proceeding with contracts or deeds, rather than assuming it can be postponed indefinitely.

The representative’s role is administrative and tax-related; it is distinct from legal representation in the purchase itself, though in practice the same firm may assist with both after agreement. Who to appoint and for which acts should be confirmed against the intended transaction and available documentation.

Buying at distance: power of attorney and apostille

Many US buyers arrange purchases without being present at every step. It is common to grant a power of attorney so a representative can sign the promissory contract, appear at the notary for the deed, and deal with registries and related formalities, provided the mandate clearly describes the powers granted and any limits.

The United States is a party to the Hague Apostille Convention. A power of attorney executed in the United States will typically require an apostille (and appropriate notarial formalities) before it can be relied on for acts in Portugal. The exact steps depend on how and where the document is signed; state-level procedures are not described here.

A generic or incomplete instrument can prevent certain acts or delay the transaction. It is prudent to align the wording of the power of attorney with the purchase timetable, any bank requirements and the acts the notary expects to see before the deed.

Due diligence before you commit

Before signing a promissory contract or paying a substantial deposit, documentary checks reduce the risk of surprises. Usual starting points include the permanent land registry certificate (certidão permanente do registo predial), which shows registered ownership and recorded encumbrances, and the caderneta predial — the Portuguese property tax document issued by the tax authority, showing the fiscal description and taxable value of the property.

It is also advisable to review any mortgages, charges or other encumbrances, the planning and licensing position (including occupancy licence where relevant), and whether tenancies, condominium debts or unlicensed works affect the asset. For new or off-plan property, the contract should reflect agreed deadlines, warranties and handover conditions in a manner that can be assessed against the documentation.

US buyers should not skip these steps because the seller or agent provides assurances from a distance. Prior legal and documentary review may reveal issues that affect price, financing or whether proceeding is advisable.

Promissory contract and public deed

The promissory purchase contract (CPCV) sets out the main terms of the sale — price, payment schedule, conditions and consequences of default — before the final transfer. It is binding in nature and should be read carefully; amendments after signing may be difficult if the other party does not agree.

The public deed (escritura pública) is executed before a notary and transfers ownership. Stamp duty and other transaction taxes are typically handled in connection with this stage, and the buyer’s details must match the NIF and documentation already in place. Registration in the land registry completes the formal record of ownership.

If the purchase is financed, the bank may impose additional documentation or insurance requirements. Aligning loan approval with the deed date before committing to the seller reduces the risk of missed deadlines under the promissory contract.

Selling later and capital gains

If you later sell property located in Portugal while you are not tax-resident there, the capital gain may be subject to taxation in Portugal. The amount, exemptions and reporting obligations depend on the facts of the sale, the acquisition history, eligible costs and the documentation available.

For owners who are tax-resident in the United States, the overall framework should be analysed case by case, taking into account both Portuguese rules on the gain and the owner’s US tax position. This page does not state rates or predict outcomes; a prior assessment can help clarify consequences before accepting an offer or signing commitments.

Deadlines for reporting and payment in Portugal apply according to the specific situation. Missing them can create additional exposure regardless of how the gain is treated elsewhere.
